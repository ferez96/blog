# AI Is the Steam Engine of the 21st Century

> *"People fear what they don't understand, and hate what they can't conquer."*

I've felt that way myself. As a backend engineer, I was skeptical of AI for a long time before I ever really used it.

But I wanted to understand it more than I wanted to fear it. And when AI started showing its true power, I couldn't help drawing a parallel to something that had done the same thing — 200 years ago.

## What Did the Steam Engine Do?

Before 1800, the limits of production were the limits of the human body. To weave more cloth, you needed more human hands. There was no other way.

The steam engine didn't make workers stronger. It decoupled productive capacity from human muscle.

The result wasn't simply "doing things faster." Factories, assembly lines, global supply chains — none of those things could have existed before. Not for lack of ideas, but for lack of the energy to run them.

And then the inevitable happened: not fewer jobs — but different jobs. Railways needed engineers. Factories needed managers. Entire professions that no one could have imagined beforehand.

## AI Is Doing the Same Thing

Before AI, the limits of software were the limits of the number of people writing code. To ship a product faster, there was only one way: add more people.

AI doesn't make developers smarter. It decouples software production capacity from human labor hours.

The evidence is hard to ignore. On SWE-Bench — the standard benchmark for real-world programming problem-solving — AI went from solving 4.4% of problems in 2023 to 71.7% in 2024. In just one year.

I can't read that number without thinking: this is not ordinary progress.

## A Trend That Cannot Be Fought

In 1811, hand-weavers in England smashed machinery because they feared losing their jobs. They weren't wrong to worry — traditional hand-weaving did disappear.

But the total number of jobs didn't shrink. It grew. And 60% of American workers today hold jobs that didn't exist in 1940.

The history of technology has never followed the arc of "machines replacing people." It has always followed this arc: replacing one specific type of work, while creating demand for a newer kind.

AI will be no different. McKinsey estimates that by 2030, at least 14% of the global workforce will need to change occupations — 375 million people. None of them can claim this trend has nothing to do with them.

## What Is Actually Changing

AI isn't replacing developers. It's replacing a specific category of developer work: boilerplate code, basic unit tests, familiar SQL queries.

What remains — deciding what to build, making architectural trade-offs, recognizing when a system is heading in the wrong direction — AI is still weak at. And that is precisely what becomes more valuable.

The bottleneck has shifted. Not from "able to write code" to "no longer needing code." But from "writing code" to "knowing what you're building and why."

## Looking Back to See What's Ahead

A hand-weaver in 1820 could not have imagined a mechanical engineer in 1850.

A switchboard operator in 1990 could not have imagined a software developer in 2000.

We are inside one of those transitions right now. Early enough to still be confused, but late enough to see which way things are heading.

I don't know where I'll stand on that map five years from now. But I know one thing: **not understanding it is not an option**.
